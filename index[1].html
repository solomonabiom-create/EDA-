Entry 01 · the basic laws

Ohm's law & Kirchhoff's voltage law

The starting point of every circuits course. First, Ohm's law: sweeping the supply across a fixed 220 Ω resistor, the current rises in a straight line and the slope of that line equals 1/R — the simulation sits exactly on the I = V/R prediction. Second, Kirchhoff's voltage law: in a series divider, the voltages across the two resistors must add up to the supply, which they do to the volt.

verified  slope = 1/R  to 4 decimals · divider voltages sum to 9.00 V

Entry 02 · a first filter

RC low-pass — smoothing a signal

A resistor and a capacitor make the simplest filter there is — the building block for cleaning up a noisy measurement. Seen in the frequency domain, it passes low frequencies and rejects high ones, with the −3 dB cut-off landing exactly where fc = 1/(2πRC) predicts. Seen in time, the output settles after a step with a smooth exponential, reaching 63% of its final value after one time constant τ = RC — the simulated curve hits that mark right on cue.

verified  −3 dB @ 1000 Hz  · 63% reached at τ = 0.16 ms

reproduce → python3 scripts/02_rc_filter.py

Entry 03 · a real design choice

Sizing an LED current-limiting resistor

A practical first design task: pick the series resistor that sets a safe current through an LED, using R = (Vsupply − VLED) / I. Using a real diode model so the LED's forward voltage is included, the simulation shows the current only flowing once the supply clears the forward voltage, and sweeping the resistor at 5 V confirms which value lands the current in the safe 10–20 mA band. The rule-of-thumb 220 Ω gives about 14 mA — just as the hand calculation says.

verified  220 Ω → 14 mA  at 5 V, inside the safe band

reproduce → python3 scripts/03_led_resistor.py
